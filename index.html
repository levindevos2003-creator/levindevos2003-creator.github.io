Who am I?

I'm Levin De Vos, a master's researcher in biophysics at KU Leuven, on the Molecular Physiology track until June 2027. Most of my days right now happen at imec, working on 2D materials and single-molecule biosensing. During my bachelor's I added a minor in Business and Innovation, mostly because the science rarely stops being interesting at the lab door.

Most of what I do sits somewhere between molecular biology, microscopy, and data. At imec that means a lot of TIRF microscopy, some microfluidics, and building small custom parts when the off-the-shelf gear doesn't fit, then trying to make sense of the numbers that come back. My bachelor's thesis had a similar flavour: I put a newer confocal microscope head to head with the lab's trusted workhorse to see where each one really earns its keep.

Away from the bench, I've run international student exchanges for IAESTE, muddled through a few interdisciplinary projects, and picked up a decent amount of AI, programming, and supply-chain thinking along the way. I also invest in the stock market: mostly ETFs, with some stock picking where I think I see something the index doesn't.

What I keep coming back to: clear results, methods you can actually trust, and teams that finish what they start.

Featured work

imec · 2026

Single-molecule biosensing

01

Master's research on detecting and following individual molecules, one at a time, on 2D materials.

The work pulls together TIRF microscopy, microfluidics, and a fair bit of hands-on fabrication: I laser-cut and 3D-print the parts I need when nothing off the shelf fits. Each of those choices changes what the final measurement can and can't tell you, which is half the challenge.

The other half is the data. Single-molecule signals are faint and noisy, so a result only counts once the quantitative analysis backs it up. That constraint keeps the project honest, and it's the part I enjoy most.

Bachelor's thesis · KU Leuven · 2025

Confocal: speed vs. resolution

02

A side-by-side of the TriScan line-scanning microscope against the Leica TCS SP8 point-scanning confocal.

Using TetraSpeck beads and fixed HeLa cells, I measured resolution and acquisition speed at 488, 561, and 638 nm. The short version: the TriScan ran up to ten times faster, though you pay for it in Z-axis sharpness, mostly because of the quadband filter and broader excitation volumes.

I also cleaned up the sample-prep protocols and built the figures that made the trade-off obvious. Handy to know before you trust the prettier picture.
